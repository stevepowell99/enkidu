- Gap in common narratives: the key story from the 1950s to late 1980s is how British produce improved via revived farmhouse traditions, organic/wholefoods movements, and a small cadre of upper‑middle countercultural actors.
- Central figure: Nicholas Saunders — translated Danish/Christiania ideas into a scalable wholefood model (Neal’s Yard), prioritising quality, accessibility, and anti‑commercial aesthetics.
- Business characteristics: upper‑class/countercultural background, not explicitly socialist, gave away successful ventures, avoided advertising, emphasised provenance and quality.
- Concrete legacies: Neal’s Yard Dairy, Borough Market (reopened 1998), The Dairy, Monmouth Coffee — each helped set standards (cheese, produce, coffee) that influenced restaurants and global reputations.
- Cultural impact: helped create demand for native British produce; by 2005 London was celebrated internationally (Gourmet issue naming St John, Neal’s Yard Dairy, Monmouth, Borough Market).
- Broader connections: Saunders linked wholefood retail to wider 60s/90s counterculture (including drug/MDMA research and communities), showing cultural practices shaped food markets.

Key quotes:
- "Fresh wholemeal rolls with butter and strong freshly ground coffee for breakfast is normal. All the food is best quality – the organic vegetables straight from farming communes." (Saunders on Christiania)
- Saunders on health‑food shops: they "would make customers feel bad because they could not recognise a mung bean." 
- "By the mid‑90s, the influence of the Dairy had grown to the point that any cheese it sold was globally significant solely by virtue of being stocked there."
